# User Research: Scope Creep in Fixed-Fee Legal Engagements
**Research Period**: Q2 2024 (April–May)
**Conducted by**: PM team, 2 weeks
**Firm Profile**: 20-person transactional M&A boutique, $8M annual revenue, 60% fixed-fee work
**Status**: Complete

---

## Research Objectives

Identify why fixed-fee M&A transactions consistently overrun budgeted hours, how scope drift currently manifests, and where intervention points exist to improve profitability without client friction.

**Specific questions**:
- How are engagement scopes currently defined and communicated?
- Where do scope additions occur and through what channels?
- Why don't lawyers flag scope drift in real-time?
- How do clients perceive scope additions: as expected, reasonable, or a bait-and-switch?

---

## Methodology

**Sample**: 5 interviews over 2 weeks
- 2 Managing Partners (Founder/Co-founder; both 18+ years M&A experience)
- 1 Senior Associate (8 years at firm; leads junior staffing)
- 1 Billing Coordinator (3 years; reconciles timesheets monthly)
- 1 Office Manager (5 years; operational; administrative context)

**Interview Format**: 45–75 minutes; semi-structured with open-ended questions about recent deals

**Recruiting**: Internal; all interviews conducted confidentially with understanding that findings would be shared as aggregate data

**Access**: Reviewed 3 engagement letters and 6 months of billing reconciliation data (anonymized)

---

## Participant Profiles

**MP-1**: Managing Partner, Founder (1998)
- 22 years at firm; exclusively M&A transactions
- Manages partner-level client relationships
- Directly involved in initial scoping for ~30% of deals
- By most recent estimate, 40% of fixed-fee deals exceed budget by 10–40%
- No formal tracking system; awareness is retrospective (at month-end billing)

**MP-2**: Managing Partner, Partner (2008)
- 16 years at firm; joined as junior associate
- Manages operations and profitability metrics
- Reviews all engagement letters pre-signature
- Expressed frustration: "We write detailed scopes on paper. Nobody references them after day one."
- Attempted spreadsheet-based scope tracking 2 years ago; abandoned after 3 months (low adoption)

**Associate-1**: Senior Associate
- 8 years at firm; staffs and supervises junior associates on all deals
- Doesn't participate in scoping conversations
- Escalates scope questions to partners via email: "Is this in scope?" (average 3–5 escalations per deal)
- Aware of scope drift but can't prevent it without partner guidance

**Billing Coordinator**: Billing and Finance
- 3 years at firm
- Monthly task: reconcile timesheets (associate invoicing) against engagement letters
- Flags discrepancies to partners; partners decide whether to adjust bills, absorb overages, or invoice client for additions
- Estimated 60% of fixed-fee deals generate "reconciliation conversations" each month

**Office Manager**: Administrative Operations
- 5 years at firm
- Oversees document management, email archives, time tracking
- Observed pattern: scope additions via email threads that never get formally documented
- "They ask for 'one more thing' in an email, someone does it, and six months later nobody remembers it was supposed to be extra."

## Key Findings

### 1. Partners Know Scope Is Creeping But Can't Quantify It in Real-Time

**Finding**: All 5 participants confirmed that scope drift happens regularly. The managing partners estimated 40–50% of fixed-fee deals exceed budget. However, this awareness only crystallizes at month-end billing reconciliation—weeks or months after the scope addition occurred.

**Evidence**:
- MP-1: "I know we're spending more than we quoted, but I don't realize it until the billing coordinator shows me the numbers at the end of the month. By then, the work is done."
- MP-2: "If I had real-time visibility into hours spent, I could have a conversation with the client in week 3, not in week 12."
- Billing Coordinator: "Every month, I email the partners with discrepancies. They say 'just bill it' or 'we'll absorb it.' I've never seen them proactively cut scope."

**Data point**: Review of 6 billing months showed average variance of 24% over budget on fixed-fee deals. Variance ranged from +8% to +47% on individual transactions.

**Implication**: Real-time tracking creates intervention opportunity that doesn't currently exist.

---

### 2. The Engagement Letter Is the Source of Truth But Nobody References It After Signing

**Finding**: All engagement letters reviewed contained detailed scope definitions (deliverables, exclusions, assumptions). Yet all 5 participants independently reported that the engagement letter "disappears" after execution—it's filed and never consulted as work evolves.

**Evidence**:
- MP-1: "I probably spend 2 hours drafting the scope. The client probably doesn't read it. We definitely don't reference it while doing the work."
- Associate-1: "I've never looked at an engagement letter for a deal I'm staffing. I don't know what was promised."
- Office Manager: "The engagement letters are in the files, but they're PDFs. People don't dig them out to check a boundary question. They just ask via email."

**Mechanism**: When a scope question arises, the partner is asked via email or Slack. The partner decides from memory, not by consulting the document. If the decision is made off-memory and the engagement letter contradicts it, nobody knows.

**Implication**: Engagement letter must be accessible, not archived. It must be the live reference for scope conversations.

---

### 3. Scope Additions Happen Through Email and Phone Calls—No Formal Change Orders

**Finding**: All scope additions observed in the research occurred through informal channels (email, phone calls, Slack). No formal change order process existed.

**Evidence**:
- MP-1: "A client calls and says 'we need antitrust due diligence on the target.' I say 'sure, that's $15K more.' They might email back 'OK,' and then we do it. Is that documented? Somewhere, maybe."
- Associate-1: "I get an email saying 'the client wants us to prepare diligence tables.' I ask if it's in scope. Someone says yes or no. But I never see a formal agreement that it's a change order."
- Office Manager: "I find scope additions by reading email threads months later when the billing coordinator asks why hours are high."

**Pattern**: Scope additions cluster as 15–20 small requests per deal, not 1–2 large ones. Each small request (1–4 hours) doesn't feel significant in real-time, but compounds.

**Implication**: Capturing small additions matters more than big ones. Change order process needs to be lightweight, not a 5-email procurement dance.

---

### 4. Associates Don't Flag Scope Drift Because They Don't Know the Boundaries

**Finding**: The senior associate and junior associates perform most of the execution work but don't participate in scoping. When scope questions arise, they escalate to partners rather than gatekeeping.

**Evidence**:
- Associate-1: "I don't know what we promised the client. I know what I was assigned to do. If someone asks for something outside that, I ask before I do it. But I can't say 'that's out of scope'—I don't know what's in scope."
- MP-2: "The associates who do the work aren't empowered to say no. They escalate every boundary question. That escalation takes time, and it slows everything down."

**Impact**: Associates absorb 2–4 hours per deal in escalation overhead (asking partners, waiting for answers, adjusting work).

**Implication**: Engagement letter and scope changes must be accessible to execution team, not just partners.

---

### 5. Clients Are Willing to Pay for Additional Scope—If Asked Early

**Finding**: This is the most important finding. When we asked "How do clients react to scope additions?", all partners said the same thing: **reactions are positive if asked early, negative if asked late.**

**Evidence**:
- MP-1: "If I call the client in week 3 and say 'we found something that'll add 20 hours, should we do it?', they usually say yes. If I tell them in week 12 that it cost more, they're angry because they didn't know."
- MP-2: "Clients care about surprises, not about additional work. They'd rather know upfront."
- Billing Coordinator: "The partners who proactively email clients about scope changes get fewer arguments at billing time. The partners who just bill it get pushback."

**Critical insight**: The economics of scope additions are not zero-sum. Clients will pay for real work. The problem is timing and visibility.

**Implication**: Product must make early visibility possible, not just late accounting.

---

### 6. Technology Adoption Barrier Is High—No IT Department, Partners Won't Learn New Tools

**Finding**: The firm has no IT department, limited technical sophistication, and high resistance to software adoption. Any solution must integrate into existing workflows (email, Word, Excel).

**Evidence**:
- MP-2: "We tried scope tracking in a spreadsheet 2 years ago. The partners didn't use it. Nobody wants another tool. We work in email and Word."
- Office Manager: "If it's not in email or our document system, partners won't see it. New software doesn't work here."
- Associate-1: "I'm already in email and Word all day. I'm not opening another app to log scope changes."

**Adoption failure case**: Spreadsheet-based tracking attempted 2 years prior. MPs estimated <20% adoption. Abandoned within 3 months.

**Implication**: Solution must be email-native or document-embedded. Standalone tool adoption is unlikely.

> "I write the scope in the letter. Then I don't read it again. The client definitely doesn't read it. Six months later, there's a 20-email thread debating what was promised." — MP-2

**On adoption barriers:**
> "I spend my day in Outlook and Word. A new software tool is just more clicking. It has to be where I already work." — Office Manager

## How This Shaped the Product

### Feature 1: Engagement Letter Import and Live Reference
Instead of archiving engagement letters, made them living documents:
- Upload engagement letter (PDF or Word) as project setup
- Extract scope section automatically (with manual correction if needed)
- Display engagement letter summary in project sidebar (always visible to team)
- Annotate engagement letter as scope questions arise ("this is X?") to build implicit documentation

### Feature 2: Email-Based Scope Change Capture
Integrated change orders into email workflow:
- Email template for scope change requests (one-click generation from email)
- Partners email the client directly; carbon copy special email address to log change order
- Automatic parsing: subject line → deliverable, cost estimate, timeline
- No separate app needed; change order appears in project tracker automatically

### Feature 3: Real-Time Hour Tracking vs. Budget
Provided visibility to partners without requiring time-log discipline:
- Integrate with firm's billing system (if available; fallback: weekly import from manual timesheets)
- Display running total: budgeted hours vs. actual hours spent by category (due diligence, drafting, review, etc.)
- Alert partner at 70% of budget with prediction of final overrun
- Simple dashboard (not a full project management tool)

### Feature 4: Associate Visibility to Engagement Scope
Made scope accessible to execution team:
- Every team member can view engagement letter and approved scope changes
- Associates can flag scope questions without leaving email (forwarded to partner)
- No "separate tool" —information is embedded in email and document workflow

### Feature 5: Lightweight Change Order Process
Designed for informal partner-client conversation, not procurement:
- Template: "We identified [deliverable] that will take [X hours] and cost [Y]. Should we proceed? Reply with yes/no/call me."
- One-sentence format; no approval workflow; no finance review
- Client reply auto-logs change order; triggers automatic scope update

### Feature 6: Pre-Billing Reconciliation Report
Shifted reconciliation earlier:
- Weekly report (instead of monthly): "Budgeted vs. actual by category"
- Highlights: which deals are on track, which are 10%+ over
- Partners see trends before month-end; can decide whether to add scope or contain hours

---

## What Didn't Get Built (Scope Decisions)

- **Full project management tool**: Partners rejected anything requiring daily interaction. Solution is lightweight, email-first.
- **Detailed time tracking by task**: Associates won't log time by 1-hour increments. We use existing billing system data only.
- **Approval workflows for scope changes**: Partners wanted friction-free; "call the client, copy the address, done."
- **Profitability analysis by deal type**: Too early; firm needs basic scope tracking before analytics.

---

## Limitations and Biases

- **Firm size**: 20 people, $8M revenue. Larger firms may have different adoption barriers and governance needs.
- **Practice area**: M&A transactional work; recurring revenue from retainer clients may have different scope dynamics.
- **No client interviews**: All data from firm perspective. Client perception of scope and pricing may differ from partners' assumptions.
- **Timing**: April-May 2024; market conditions (M&A volume, client sophistication) fluctuate.

---

**Next Steps**: Follow up with 2–3 firms in adjacent practice areas (bankruptcy, corporate) to validate whether findings generalize. Conduct 1 client interview to validate "clients are willing to pay if asked early" assumption.
